Station 01 · The NucleusT ≈ 100 million Ka red giant, billions of years ago

Tin is born lucky

Every property that eventually makes cassiterite minable is already written into the tin nucleus before Earth exists.

Tin is element 50, and 50 is one of nuclear physics' magic numbers: a completely filled shell of protons, the nuclear equivalent of a noble gas configuration. A magic proton count makes an unusually stable nucleus, and stability has a spectacular consequence: tin has ten stable isotopes, more than any other element in the periodic table. Nothing else comes close.

Most of that tin was forged by the s-process (slow neutron capture) in the pulsing envelopes of dying AGB stars: an iron seed nucleus quietly swallows neutrons over thousands of years, beta-decays its way up the chart of nuclides, and stalls comfortably at the magic ledge of Z = 50. Supernovae and neutron-star mergers (the r-process) top up the heavy isotopes. The debris seeded the solar nebula, and about 4.567 billion years ago some of it condensed into the planet under your boots.

Never forget

Tin has a magic nucleus and a two-faced valence. The nucleus explains why there is tin at all; the valence explains where it goes. Everything downstream is bookkeeping.

Station 02 · The IonSn²⁺ vs Sn⁴⁺the master switch

The two faces of tin

One element, two ions, two utterly different fates. This single toggle decides whether a granite province is a tin field or a dud.

In an oxidised (magnetite-series) magma, tin sits as Sn⁴⁺ with an ionic radius of about 0.69 Å, nearly a twin of Ti⁴⁺ (0.605 Å). Titanite, magnetite, ilmenite, rutile and biotite crystallising early all quietly accept Sn⁴⁺ into Ti and Fe³⁺ sites. The tin is sequestered, grain by grain, scattered through the pluton at a few ppm each, and the magma reaches the end of crystallisation with nothing left to give. That province will never make a tin deposit no matter how big its granites are.

In a reduced (ilmenite-series) magma, tin is Sn²⁺: large, asymmetric because of its stereochemically active 5s² lone pair, and rejected by essentially every liquidus mineral. It behaves as a classic incompatible element, exactly like Rb, Li, Cs, F, Nb and Ta. Each crystal that forms leaves the shrinking melt slightly richer in tin. This is why redox is the first-order fertility filter for tin worldwide, and why the tin-bearing biotite granites of the Younger Granite suite are the reduced end of the family.

Never forget

Oxidised magma buries its tin alive inside titanium minerals. Reduced magma lets tin run free in the melt until the very end. Fertile tin granite = reduced granite.

Station 03 · The EngineT ≈ 1200 °C40 to 80 km deep · 213 to 141 Ma

A continent drifts over a flame

The Younger Granites are anorogenic: no collision, no subduction, no mountain belt. Just a stationary thermal anomaly and a moving plate, stamping ring complexes like a slow typewriter.

Through the Jurassic, the West African lithosphere drifted across a long-lived sub-lithospheric hot zone. Hot mantle melts ponded and underplated the base of the Pan-African crust, and that heat partially melted the ancient basement above it. The result was A-type (anorogenic, alkaline, anhydrous-ish) silicic magma: hot, halogen-rich, iron-rich, water-poor at birth, and generated inside stable continental crust rather than at any plate boundary.

The evidence is written on the map. The province is a roughly north-south chain of over 50 ring complexes running from Aïr in Niger down through Zinder to the Jos Plateau and beyond, and the emplacement ages young southward, from around 213 Ma near the border to about 141 Ma at the southern tip. In Nigeria the crustal address matters too: the complexes punch through the ~600 Ma Pan-African Older Granites and Precambrian migmatite-gneiss, which is why the map contrast between smooth-textured Younger Granite and the deformed basement is so readable in imagery and geophysics around Toro.

Never forget

A-type magmatism is a heat event, not a collision. The chain of complexes is a plate's flight recorder, and the Jos Plateau is the frame where erosion depth and mineralised roof zones coincide.

Station 04 · The ConcentrationT ≈ 900 → 700 °Chigh-level magma chamber, 3 to 6 km

The great squeeze: Rayleigh fractionation

Ore deposits are arithmetic before they are geology. Here is the arithmetic, live.

The magma arrives in its shallow chamber with a boring 2 to 3 ppm tin. Crystallisation begins: plagioclase, alkali feldspar, quartz, biotite. Each of these rejects Sn²⁺ (and Nb, Ta, Li, Rb, F, H₂O), so every tonne of crystal removed leaves the remaining melt richer. The bookkeeping is Rayleigh fractionation:

Cmelt = C₀ · F(D−1) C₀ = starting concentration · F = melt fraction remaining · D = bulk partition coefficient (how much the crystals want the element; incompatible means D ≪ 1)

Three practical consequences, each one an exploration instinct:

  • The last melt matters most. Mineralisation clings to the youngest, most evolved, most albite- and topaz-rich granite phase in a complex, never the big early biotite granite mass itself.
  • Fractionation indices are vectors. Falling K/Rb, rising Rb/Sr, rising Rb, Li, Cs, F, Sn, W, Nb, Ta: whole-rock geochemistry literally points toward the fertile cupola.
  • Small is beautiful. A modest, highly evolved stock beats a batholith of unevolved granite. Tonnage of granite means nothing; degree of evolution means everything.
Never forget

C = C₀·F(D−1) is the only equation a tin explorer must love. Ore is what happens when D stays tiny while F collapses.

Station 05 · The EnablerT ≈ 700 → 650 °Cthe melt that refused to die

Fluorine keeps the melt alive

Extreme fractionation should be impossible: a normal granite melt freezes solid long before F reaches 5%. Fluorine is the cheat code.

A silicate melt is a tangle of polymerised SiO₄ tetrahedra; as it cools and crystallises it stiffens until it locks. Fluorine breaks the tangle. F⁻ substitutes for bridging oxygens, depolymerising the network, which does three enormous things at once:

  • Drops the solidus by well over 100 °C, so the residual melt stays liquid down toward ~650 °C and can keep fractionating far beyond a normal granite's death.
  • Slashes viscosity, so the small residual melt fraction can segregate, migrate, and pool in the roof instead of freezing in place between crystals.
  • Complexes with high-field-strength elements, helping keep Sn, Nb and Ta dissolved to absurd concentrations in the final melt.

You can hold the proof in your hand at Toro. Topaz [Al₂SiO₄(F,OH)₂] and fluorite [CaF₂] are frozen fluorine budgets: a granite carrying magmatic topaz is a granite that ran the full course of enrichment. Zinnwaldite and Li-siderophyllite micas tell the same story in Li-F language. This is precisely why your LV 029 topaz granite reads as a priority target: topaz granite is not a rock type, it is a confession.

Fluorine is the antifreeze and the courier. No fluorine, no extreme fractionation; no extreme fractionation, no tin granite. Topaz in outcrop is fluorine's signed receipt.

Station 06 · The Architecturecaldera → ring dyke → plutonsurface to ~4 km

Anatomy of a ring complex

Every Younger Granite complex is a collapsed volcano caught in the act. The plumbing controls where the metal ends up.

The sequence repeats across the province like a liturgy: first voluminous rhyolite and ignimbrite erupt from a shallow chamber; the emptied roof founders along steep ring fractures (caldera collapse); magma squirts up the fractures as ring dykes of granite porphyry; and finally the deeper chamber crystallises as biotite granite plutons, with the most evolved melts rising last into domed cupolas under the roof. Peralkaline members (arfvedsonite and riebeckite granites, like the famous Nb-rich albite-riebeckite granite of the Liruei complex) and peraluminous biotite granites can share one complex, but it is the reduced biotite to topaz-albite granites that carry the Sn-Zn story.

Never forget

Drill the dome, not the mass. Cupolas, roof pendants and stockscheider textures are the address of the orebody; the main pluton is only its biography.

Station 07 · The ExhaleT ≈ 650 → 400 °Cthe magmatic-hydrothermal transition

Second boiling: the magma exhales a metal-bearing brine

Crystals cannot carry the tin. A fluid can. Here the story switches vehicles.

Water, like tin, is incompatible: as anhydrous feldspar and quartz crystallise, H₂O piles up in the residual melt until it saturates. The melt then exsolves an aqueous fluid while still crystallising, a process called second (resurgent) boiling. The exsolving bubbles do not wander; they are buoyant, and they collect exactly where you would predict: under the cupola roof, building pressure against the carapace until it fails and stockworks, sheeted veins and breccias rip through the roof zone.

That fluid is no ordinary water. It is a hot, acidic, saline, F- and B-bearing brine, and chloride is the taxi: under reduced, acidic conditions tin dissolves and travels as Sn(II)-chloride complexes (SnCl⁺, SnCl₂⁰), reaching hundreds to thousands of ppm Sn in the fluid, orders of magnitude beyond any melt.

Sn²⁺ + Cl⁻ → SnCl⁺  ·  SnCl⁺ + Cl⁻ → SnCl₂⁰ Hot + acidic + saline + reduced = tin stays dissolved and mobile. Remove any one of those four conditions and cassiterite falls out of solution. Deposition is simply transport with one leg kicked away.

Meanwhile a quieter, parallel plot resolves itself: niobium and tantalum never board the fluid. Nb⁵⁺ and Ta⁵⁺ are too highly charged to travel well in chloride brines, so they crystallise directly from the last albite-rich melt as disseminated magmatic columbite, the style classically developed in the Jos-Bukuru biotite granites. One granite, two delivery services: Nb-Ta ships magmatic, Sn ships hydrothermal. This is why columbite haloes are pluton-wide and low grade while cassiterite lodes are tight, structurally controlled and high grade, and why both meet again only in the placers.

Never forget

Chloride is tin's taxi; fluorine paid for the trip; the cupola is the taxi rank. And remember the fork in the road: columbite stays magmatic, cassiterite goes hydrothermal.

Station 08 · The TrapT ≈ 450 → 300 °Cgreisen zone, roof fractures

Greisenisation: the rock that eats its own acid

Cassiterite precipitates the moment the brine loses any one of its four life-support conditions. The wall rock itself pulls the trigger.

As the brine invades the cupola carapace and fractured roof, it attacks feldspar. This is greisenisation, and it is beautifully self-defeating for the fluid:

3 KAlSi₃O₈ + 2 H⁺ → KAl₃Si₃O₁₀(OH)₂ + 6 SiO₂ + 2 K⁺ Feldspar + acid → muscovite + quartz. The reaction consumes H⁺, so pH rises; rising pH destabilises SnCl⁺; cassiterite precipitates in the very rock the acid is destroying. The trap builds itself.
SnCl₂⁰ + 2 H₂O → SnO₂ (cassiterite) + 2 HCl + H₂↑ The net deposition reaction: Sn(II) in the fluid is oxidised to Sn(IV) in the crystal, hydrogen escapes, and the taxi (HCl) is dismissed.

The zoned aftermath

The finished system is concentrically ordered, and the ordering is an exploration compass. From the cupola outward and upward: albitised apogranite with disseminated cassiterite-columbite → massive greisen (quartz-mica-topaz-fluorite ± cassiterite ± wolframite) → greisen-bordered quartz-cassiterite veins and Sn-Zn lodes (the Rayfield style, with sphalerite joining outboard) → distal fluorite-sulfide fringe. Your LV 016 greisen and LV 029 topaz granite at Toro are two adjacent rungs of exactly this ladder, which is what makes their pairing more convincing than either sample alone.

Never forget

Greisen is a suicide-note written by the fluid: the acid that carried the tin is consumed by the feldspar it attacks, and the tin drops on the spot. Zoning = frozen chemistry gradient = compass.

Station 09 · The Crystaltetragonal · P4₂/mnmrutile structure

Cassiterite: a fortress of octahedra

The atom that spent 170 million years being a misfit finally finds a lattice built exactly for it, and it never leaves.

Cassiterite is SnO₂ in the rutile structure: tetragonal, space group P4₂/mnm, a ≈ 4.738 Å, c ≈ 3.188 Å. Each Sn⁴⁺ sits in an octahedron of six oxygens; the octahedra share edges in chains along the c-axis and corners between chains. Short, strong, highly covalent Sn-O bonds make the structure dense and nearly indestructible, and that fact alone writes the entire final act of the story (and the last century of Nigerian mining history).

Station 10 · DaylightT ≈ 30 °CJos Plateau surface · last ~70 Myr

The granite dies; the cassiterite doesn't

Tens of millions of years of tropical weathering demolish the host rock grain by grain. One mineral refuses to negotiate.

Deep chemical weathering under a savanna climate turns feldspar to kaolinite, biotite to vermiculite and iron oxides, and the plateau surface to laterite. The greisen lodes and mineralised apogranite decompose in place, and their cassiterite (with columbite, monazite, zircon and thorite as travelling companions) is released into the regolith. Concentration now happens a second time, hydraulically:

  • Eluvial placers: residual enrichment directly over and downslope of the lodes; the fines wash away, the heavies stay. Grades highest, transport distance zero: eluvial cassiterite is a drill vector pointing straight down.
  • Alluvial placers: streams sweep the heavies into channel gravels, inside bends, bedrock riffles and the base-of-gravel contact ("the wash").
  • Deep leads: the plateau's signature trick: Neogene Newer Basalt flows buried entire Miocene river systems, sealing rich pre-basalt channel placers under lava. Some of the richest ground ever worked on the plateau was mined from under basalt.
Never forget

The province concentrated its tin twice: once by fractionation in fire, once by gravity in water. The placer map is a blurred photocopy of the lode map; learn to read it backwards.

Station 11 · The Payoffregional → district → prospectthe model, inverted into a search plan

The exploration model: run the story in reverse

Every station you just descended through left a measurable fingerprint. Exploration is the story read backwards, coarse to fine.

Regional scale · find the fertile complex

  • Aeromagnetics: ring complexes draw circular anomaly patterns; crucially, the reduced ilmenite-series biotite granites are magnetite-poor, so the fertile phases tend to sit in subdued magnetic lows inside the ring, while ring dykes and basalt flows ring them with highs. (Your Yelwa Sheet 118 CET lineament work is exactly this filter in action.)
  • Radiometrics: evolved granites scream in K, U and Th. Better: ratios. Albitisation and greisenisation redistribute K, so Th/K and U/K highs over a granite cupola outperform raw counts. Ternary K-Th-U imaging paints apogranite roofs in unmistakable colours.
  • Remote sensing: Sentinel-2 and ASTER band ratios for muscovite-topaz (Al-OH) alteration and for the kaolinised, iron-stained weathering caps; circular drainage anomalies betray ring structures even under regolith.

District scale · find the fertile phase

  • Lithogeochemistry: map every granite phase and plot K/Rb (falling toward fertility, into the low hundreds), Rb/Sr (rising), Sn, Li, F, Rb, Cs, Nb, Ta (all rising). The most evolved phase wins; everything else is context.
  • Stream sediments + heavy mineral concentrates: Sn-W-Nb-Ta-Li anomalies with panned cassiterite counts; walk anomalies upstream to the eluvial shadow, then to source. Old placer paddocks are free, pre-industrial stream-sediment surveys: respect them as data.
  • Mineral witnesses in float and outcrop: topaz, fluorite (often purple), zinnwaldite books, chessboard albite, greisen borders on quartz veins, stockscheider pegmatite. Each is one rung of the Station 8 ladder.

Prospect scale · find the cupola roof

  • Soil grids (Sn, W, As, Li, F) over the geochemical shoulder; ground magnetics to trace the granite-basement contact and roof pendants under cover.
  • Trenching across greisen zones to establish vein density and true widths; then scout RC/DD holes angled through the cupola contact, because grade lives in the top tens of metres of apogranite and the first hundred metres of roof.
  • Log the zoning direction in every hole: albitisation intensity, greisen mineralogy, Sn:Zn ratio. The gradient tells you whether you are drilling toward or away from the engine.

Notice what this means for Toro: a topaz-granite sample (LV 029) and a greisen sample (LV 016) inside one licence are not two anomalies, they are two adjacent chapters of Station 8 caught in place, and the model predicts a cupola roof between and beneath them. That prediction, not the assays alone, is what a drill program should test.

Never forget

Geophysics finds the complex, geochemistry finds the phase, mineralogy finds the roof, and the drill tests the prediction. Never let a tool answer a question that belongs to a different scale.

Station 12 · Memory anchorstake these with you

The Five F's of a fertile tin granite

If you retain nothing else, retain these five words. Any granite scoring five out of five deserves your field season.

F

Fractionated

Extreme: last-phase apogranite, K/Rb collapsing, Rb-Li-Cs soaring. C = C₀·F^(D−1) pushed to the wall.

F

Fluorine-rich

Topaz, fluorite, Li-F micas. The antifreeze that let fractionation run and the ligand that helped carry the metal.

F

FeO-reduced

Ilmenite-series, low fO₂. Keeps tin as misfit Sn²⁺ so it survives to the residual melt instead of dying inside titanite.

F

Fluid-saturated

Second boiling reached; brine exsolved; chloride taxis loaded. Stockscheider and greisen are the receipts.

F

Focused

A cupola under an intact roof, fractures to fail, traps to fill. Chemistry without architecture is just an anomaly.

The whole story in one breath

A magic-nucleus atom forged in a dying star waits four billion years in ordinary crust, until a hotspot under drifting Jurassic Africa melts that crust into reduced, fluorine-charged A-type magma; rejected as a misfit Sn²⁺ by every crystal, it is concentrated a thousandfold into the last melt of a cupola, exhaled in a chloride brine at second boiling, and dropped as cassiterite when greisen reactions devour the acid that carried it; too hard, too heavy and too inert to die with its granite, it is concentrated a second time by rivers, buried under basalt, and finally shaken to the bottom of a pan on the Jos Plateau.

Test yourself: close this page and tell the story out loud, station by station, to a junior geologist (or to Kenny). If you can narrate the atom's journey without notes, you own the model, and every survey you design afterwards is just asking the atom where it stopped.
